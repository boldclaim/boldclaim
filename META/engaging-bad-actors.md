# Engaging Bad Actors — A Field Guide

Most advice on pushing back online is written by people who've read about trolls. This is written from months of active engagement across Reddit, Twitter, and comment sections, hundreds of replies posted, multiple own-goal statistics caught before publishing, coordinated networks mapped across communities, and one opponent who cited a questionnaire item from the wrong study and nearly blew up an entire campaign.

The audience for your reply is never the opponent. It's the hundreds of people scrolling past who haven't made up their mind yet. Every reply is a performance for the silent readers who never comment but form opinions. The opponent is a prop. Making the method visible inoculates future readers against the same trick.

## Who you're actually dealing with

"Troll" is useless as a category. Bad-faith actors fall into distinct types, and each requires a different weapon. Misidentifying the type wastes your strongest reply on the wrong target.

### The Barroom Expert

Two-word answers to complex questions. "Just deport them." "Common sense." "Cope." They contribute nothing, but their brevity makes them look confident to readers.

The trap: writing a five-paragraph rebuttal to a two-word comment. That makes you look try-hard and them look unbothered. Match their energy. The brevity of your kill IS the humiliation.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them:* You list three legal obstacles to deportation (stateless persons, unclear identity, dependent children). They reply: "Just deport them."
> 
> *Counter:* "Three legal questions. Your answer is two words. That ratio tells the reader everything they need to know."
> 
> *Why it works:* Three lines, not three paragraphs. You didn't explain why they're wrong (every reader already sees it). You pointed at the gap between your effort and theirs, and let the reader draw the conclusion. Anything longer would dignify the two words.
> 
> *Their next move:* "Look how triggered they are by common sense. Wrote a whole paragraph to argue against 'deport criminals.' Normal people get it." They'll reframe your reply as evidence of being rattled.
> *Preempt:* Your reply was three lines, not a paragraph. The "triggered" frame only sticks if you over-invested. If you matched their brevity, their counter falls flat because there's no visible effort to mock. The preemption IS the brevity.

### The Pseudo-Methodologist

Throws around "correlation isn't causation" or "cherry-picking" without understanding either concept. Usually mocks your credentials in the same breath. The giveaway: they never provide sources themselves.

The weapon: quote their own words back at them. They almost always violate their own standard within the same comment. Point at the self-contradiction, walk away. Don't explain why it's wrong (that's teacher voice). The reader sees it.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them:* "Correlation isn't causation. Maybe criminal personalities just don't want to work, ever thought about that?"
> 
> *Counter:* "You say correlation isn't causation, then in the next sentence claim criminal personalities cause unemployment. Without a source. Your own rule ate your own argument."
> 
> *Why it works:* You didn't bring new evidence. You didn't cite a study. You used their own sentence against the sentence before it. They can't dismiss your source because the source is them. The reader watches someone trip over their own feet.
> 
> *Their next move:* "That's not what I meant and you know it. You're twisting my words instead of addressing the actual numbers." They'll accuse you of bad-faith reading.
> *Preempt:* You quoted them verbatim. Include the exact quote in your reply so the reader can see you didn't twist anything. The accusation of word-twisting collapses when the original words are right there. Don't paraphrase, copy-paste.

### The Selective Empath

"I care about the victims, not the perpetrators," but then spends three paragraphs discussing the perpetrator's passport, ethnicity, or immigration status. Never mentions prevention, victim support, faster courts, or shelters.

The weapon: point out what they're NOT talking about. Don't argue the substance (they'll drag you into an empathy contest you can't win without sounding heartless). Expose the framing.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them:* "I don't care where they're from. I care about the victims. But when 40% of suspects are foreign nationals at 15% of the population, something is clearly wrong with who we're letting in."
> 
> *Counter:* "You say you care about victims. Your entire comment is about who we let in. Not a word about shelters, faster courts, prevention programmes, or victim support. Three paragraphs about passports, zero about protection."
> 
> *Why it works:* You didn't deny the statistic. You didn't argue immigration policy. You held a mirror to the gap between their stated priority (victims) and their actual focus (passports). The reader sees the mismatch without you having to name it.
> 
> *Their next move:* "Deportation IS victim protection. Removing the threat prevents future victims. You're the one who doesn't care about victims, because your 'prevention' approach leaves dangerous people on the street." They'll redefine deportation as protection.
> *Preempt:* Anticipate this by including one line: "Deportation removes one person. Prevention reduces the conditions that produce the next one." This frames the choice before they can redefine it. The reader now sees two approaches instead of "protection vs. no protection."

### The Party Parrot

Repeats partisan talking points as if they were data. This happens across every political ecosystem. In Germany, AfD parliamentary questions get cited as statistics. In the US, think-tank briefs from Heritage or Cato get quoted as peer-reviewed science. In the UK, newspaper editorials become "studies show." The mechanism is always the same: a political instrument treated as a data point.

The weapon: name the source. This works because the reader can verify the origin.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them:* "Studies show that immigrant crime is massively undercounted. Just look at the first-name data from the parliamentary inquiry."
> 
> *Counter:* "That 'study' is a parliamentary question filed by a far-right party. It's a political instrument, not a dataset. The same party voted against child protection legislation three times. They don't want the numbers to go down. They want to use them."
> 
> *Why it works:* You named the actual origin (party propaganda, not research), which the reader can verify. Then the drive-by: the party's own voting record on the topic they claim to care about, embedded in the same sentence, not as a separate paragraph. The juxtaposition does the work.
> 
> *Their next move:* "Attack the source instead of the data. Classic. The numbers are the numbers regardless of who asked the question." They'll separate the data from the instrument.
> *Preempt:* The counter isn't that the data is wrong. The counter is that a parliamentary question isn't data collection, it's political framing. Add one line: "Parliamentary questions don't collect data. They request selective disclosure. The methodology determines what you find." This blocks the "numbers are numbers" retreat.

### The Deflector

Responds to facts with personal attacks ("are you having a breakdown?"), profile mockery, or topic changes. Common in threads where the deflection serves to avoid answering uncomfortable questions.

The weapon: restate the unanswered question. Don't engage the attack. The juxtaposition between their deflection and your substance does the work.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them:* You ask which politicians in their party have been charged with sexual offences. They reply: "Are you having some kind of breakdown? Get help."
> 
> *Counter:* "The question was about politicians charged with sexual offences. You answered with a mental health diagnosis. For the readers: [Name], convicted 2019. [Name], investigation ongoing. [Name], resigned after leaked messages. But sure, I'm the one with a problem."
> 
> *Why it works:* You ignored the personal attack entirely. You restated the question (so the reader remembers what was asked), then answered it yourself with facts. The closer turns their attack into your punchline. The reader sees: they deflected, you delivered.
> 
> *Their next move:* "So now anyone who disagrees with you is a paedophile defender? This is exactly the kind of smear that makes normal people distrust the left." They'll reframe your facts as a smear campaign.
> *Preempt:* You listed names, charges, and outcomes, not opinions. The preemption is specificity: convicted dates, ongoing investigations, resignation reasons. The more specific your facts, the harder the "smear" frame sticks. "Smear" implies vagueness. Precision makes that label absurd.

### The Sealioner

Asks questions endlessly, never engages substantively. Looks reasonable ("I'm just asking questions!") but the purpose is exhaustion, not understanding. One user across multiple sub-threads owned a heat pump, solar panels, and an electric car, and argued against all three. Zero substantive engagement in nine comments.

The tell: high comment count, zero new information offered, every response is another question. After three rounds without new arguments, name the tactic and exit.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them:* (Comment 1) "But what about the economic impact?" (Comment 2) "OK but have you considered the cultural angle?" (Comment 3) "Sure, but what does that mean for local communities?" (Comment 4) "Interesting, but isn't that just one region?"
> 
> *Counter:* "Four comments, four topic shifts, zero sources. You're not asking to understand. You're asking to exhaust. That's called [sealioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sealioning). Sources are in my first reply. Read them or don't."
> 
> *Why it works:* You counted the pattern (four comments, four shifts) so the reader can verify it by scrolling up. You named the tactic with a link, which gives curious readers a framework. Then you exited. The sealioner's power is your continued engagement. Withdrawing it ends the game.
> 
> *Their next move:* "I asked a simple question and got called a sealion. Can't even have a conversation anymore." Victim mode, reframing your exit as censorship.
> *Preempt:* Your exit already included "Sources are in my first reply. Read them or don't." This blocks the "they refused to engage" frame because you pointed at existing answers. The reader can scroll and confirm you already answered. Exit with a pointer, not just a label.

### The Convinced Sceptic

Argues point-by-point, concedes when proven wrong, stays frustrated but honest. Gives ground when the data is clear, argues back when it isn't.

This is NOT a bad actor. Use factual mode, not exposure mode. Concessions build credibility here. "You're right, the overrepresentation is real. But what explains it is the decisive question." These conversations are the most valuable for readers because they see genuine dialogue.

### The Coordinated Network

Not a type but a pattern. The same accounts post the same talking points across communities within hours. One account posts a study, the others amplify with "you're downplaying it!" framing. Scores vary wildly by community (low engagement on politically aligned spaces, high on mixed ones). The audience determines reach, not content quality.

Document the network. It helps future sessions and allows you to call out coordination publicly.

## The rhetorical tricks taxonomy

Bad actors recycle the same playbook. Recognising the trick by name, quickly, lets you name it for readers instead of getting dragged into the substance the trick is designed to obscure.

**Cherry-picking.** Showing one stat while hiding the full picture.

> *Them:* "Page 51 of the study proves it's cultural."
> *Counter:* "You stopped reading at page 51. The study didn't. Pages 52-58 attribute the differential to legal status and socioeconomic factors."
> *Dissection:* You didn't bring a different study. You read more of theirs. The reader thinks: "If they had to stop at page 51, what's on page 52?"
> *Their next move:* "The authors' interpretation doesn't change the data. The numbers on page 51 stand on their own."
> *Preempt:* Quote the authors' own conclusion, not just the data. "The authors themselves write: '[exact quote from conclusion].' You're disagreeing with the researchers who collected your data."

**Survivorship bias.** "I was poor and I made it, so poverty isn't an excuse."

> *Counter:* "Your biography is one data point. The study covers seven years across an entire region. Your life is N=1."
> *Dissection:* Short, no hostility, mathematically inarguable. The reader who was nodding along at the personal story suddenly sees the scale mismatch.
> *Their next move:* "So lived experience means nothing? Only your precious studies count? This is exactly why normal people don't trust academics."
> *Preempt:* Don't dismiss their experience. Reframe the relationship: "Your experience is real. It's just not a sample. The study includes thousands of stories like yours AND the ones that went differently." This blocks the "elitist" frame by including their story in the data rather than opposing it to the data.

**False equivalence.** "Both sides are equally bad."

> *Them:* "Antifa is just as violent as the far right."
> *Counter:* "200+ deaths from right-wing violence since 1990. Under 5 from the left. Calling those equal isn't a fact. It's the argument you're trying to smuggle past the reader."
> *Dissection:* You named the specific asymmetry (200 vs 5), then named the trick ("smuggle past the reader"). The reader can verify the number. The equivalence collapses.
> *Their next move:* "One death is too many. You're comparing body counts like it's a competition." They'll reframe your asymmetry as callousness.
> *Preempt:* Anchor in purpose, not comparison: "I'm not ranking deaths. I'm showing that calling these equivalent hides a 40:1 ratio. Hiding that ratio is a choice, and it has consequences for where we focus resources."

**Gish gallop.** Flooding with so many claims that no one can rebut them all.

> *Them:* Seven claims in one comment, each requiring a paragraph to debunk.
> *Counter:* Pick the most obviously wrong one, destroy it in two lines, then: "That's one of your seven claims, and it's wrong. Why should anyone trust the other six?"
> *Dissection:* You refused the trap (chasing all seven). By destroying one cleanly, you cast doubt on all of them. The reader thinks: "If that one was wrong..."
> *Their next move:* "You only addressed one point. What about the other six? Convenient." They'll reframe your discipline as evasion.
> *Preempt:* Name the tactic before they can: "You made seven claims. That's called a Gish gallop, and the point of it is that nobody can rebut all seven. So I picked the most checkable one. It's wrong. The reader can decide what that means for the other six."

**Appeal to common sense.** "Everyone knows that..." substitutes a feeling of obviousness for evidence.

> *Counter:* "Everyone knew the earth was flat, too. What does the data say?"
> *Dissection:* One line. The historical parallel makes "everyone knows" sound silly without you having to call the person silly. The redirect to data reframes the conversation from feelings to evidence.
> *Their next move:* "You're comparing flat earth to immigration concerns? That's exactly the kind of dismissive arrogance that makes people vote right."
> *Preempt:* The parallel isn't about the topic, it's about the phrase. If they call it dismissive, pivot: "I'm not comparing topics. I'm pointing out that 'everyone knows' has never been evidence. What does the data show?" Stay on the method, not the analogy.

**Motte-and-bailey.** Advance a strong claim, then retreat to a weaker defensible one when challenged.

> *Them:* (Comment 1) "Immigrants are destroying this country." (Comment 2, after pushback) "I'm just saying we need to have a conversation about integration."
> *Counter:* "You started at 'destroying this country' and retreated to 'let's have a conversation.' Those aren't the same claim. Which one do you actually believe?"
> *Dissection:* You quoted both positions side by side. The reader sees the retreat. The closing question forces them to pick one, and either choice is uncomfortable: the strong claim is indefensible, the weak one abandons their original point.
> *Their next move:* "My position evolved as the conversation progressed. That's called being open-minded. You should try it." They'll reframe the retreat as intellectual growth.
> *Preempt:* The quotes do the work. "Evolved in 20 minutes from 'destroying the country' to 'let's talk about integration.' That's not evolution, that's a retreat under pressure." Timestamp the shift if possible. Speed of "evolution" is the tell.

**Nutpicking.** Finding the worst example from the other side and treating it as representative.

> *Them:* Shares a screenshot of one unhinged tweet: "This is what the left actually believes."
> *Counter:* "One person with 12 followers. That's not a movement, that's a screenshot."
> *Dissection:* The follower count does all the work. It reframes "this is what they believe" into "this is what one nobody said." The reader immediately recalibrates.
> *Their next move:* "Fine, here's another one. And another. How many examples do you need?" They'll flood with more screenshots.
> *Preempt:* Stay on the method, not the examples: "You can find a screenshot for any position. I can find one that says the earth is flat. The question isn't whether individuals exist. The question is what the actual numbers show." Redirect from anecdotes to data before the flood starts.

**Topic-hopping.** When losing on one point, jumping to another. The tell is three subjects in three comments.

> *Counter:* "We started on housing policy. You moved to immigration. Now you're on energy. Pick one and finish it."
> *Dissection:* Naming the three topics in sequence makes the pattern visible. "Pick one" is a command, not a request. The reader sees someone running from the argument.
> *Their next move:* "These issues are all connected. You can't discuss immigration without discussing economics and energy. You're the one trying to narrow the conversation."
> *Preempt:* "Connected, sure. But you didn't connect them, you abandoned each one when it got uncomfortable. Pick the connection you want to make and make it. I'll wait." Forces them to build an actual argument instead of hopping.

## Five modes: the default is silence

The default response to a bad-faith comment is no response. Most comments don't deserve one. Replying is a decision that should clear a bar, not a reflex. Every reply you write is material the opponent can work with: quote out of context, screenshot, use to provoke your next reply. Silence gives them nothing.

The bar: reply only when (1) the comment has a falsifiable claim, (2) it's visible enough that silent readers might believe it unchallenged, and (3) you can add something no one else in the thread has said. If any of those three fails, scroll past.

When you do reply, it operates in one of four active modes. The mistake is mixing them, or only knowing two. Using exposure on a good-faith sceptic makes you look like a bully. Using bridge mode on a Barroom Expert makes you look like you're lecturing an empty chair. But those aren't the only options, and [research on counter-speech effectiveness](https://dangerousspeech.org/considerations-for-successful-counterspeech/) consistently shows that hostile replies backfire (they escalate and discourage allies from joining), while empathy, humour, and solidarity outperform both lecturing and attacking.

The choice isn't about how angry you are. It's about who's reading and what they need to see.

### Bridge (build a case)

Full sourcing, nuance, concessions where warranted. You're building a case for the undecided reader, not scoring points against the opponent. Concessions are a tool here, not a weakness: acknowledging what's true in the opponent's position signals to readers that you're trustworthy enough to follow when you disagree.

**When to use it:** The opponent is arguing in good faith (even if wrong). The thread has undecided readers. The topic is complex enough that a short reply would oversimplify.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them:* "Foreign nationals are 15% of the population but 35% of suspects. That's a 2.3x overrepresentation. The numbers speak for themselves."
> 
> *Bridge reply:* "The overrepresentation is real and I won't pretend otherwise. But 'the numbers speak for themselves' skips the part where we ask what's driving them. A [seven-year study in Schleswig-Holstein](https://kfn.de/wp-content/uploads/Forschungsberichte/FB_161.pdf) compared refugees with the same origin and age profile but different legal status. Those with residence permits and work authorisation showed dramatically lower crime rates than those stuck in limbo without either. Same people, same background, different legal situation, different outcome. The strongest predictor wasn't where they came from. It was whether they were allowed to work."
> 
> *Why this mode:* The opponent made a real point with real numbers. A three-line dismissal would look evasive. The concession ("the overrepresentation is real") earns the right to reframe.

### Expose (make the emptiness visible)

3-5 lines. No concessions. No teacher voice. Casual tone, like you barely had to try. You're not building a case; you're pointing at something the reader can already see. The opponent's own words, their own contradiction, their own retreat.

**When to use it:** The opponent is acting in bad faith. Their argument is thin enough that length would dignify it. They're deflecting, attacking personally, or recycling talking points with no substance.

> **Example (same topic, different opponent)**
> 
> *Them:* "35% of suspects. 15% of population. Do the math, genius."
> 
> *Expose reply:* "I did. Crime rate for refugees with work permits is close to the general average. Crime rate for those banned from working is much higher. Same people, different legal status. The math works, you just stopped too early."
> 
> *Why this mode:* "Do the math, genius" is a Barroom Expert move. They contributed nothing, just restated numbers with attitude. Four lines, matches their energy, drops the key fact as a drive-by, and closes with their own phrase turned against them.

### Disarm (defuse through humour or questions)

Neither lecturing nor attacking. Humour, Socratic questions, or unexpected reframing that pulls the ground out from under the argument without confrontation. [Research shows](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/03637751.2022.2097284) humour-based corrections work better than logic-based ones on people who already believe the misinformation, because humour bypasses the defensive reflex that makes people dig in when corrected directly.

**When to use it:** The thread is heated and another serious reply would just add fuel. The opponent's position is absurd enough that pointing at it sideways is more effective than engaging it head-on. You want to draw attention to your reply (humour gets shared and upvoted more than sourced rebuttals).

> **Example (same topic, different approach)**
> 
> *Them:* "35% of suspects. 15% of population. The numbers speak for themselves."
> 
> *Disarm reply:* "Interesting. People from the same country show completely different crime rates depending on whether they're allowed to work. If it's about where they come from, why does a work permit change the number?"
> 
> *Why this mode:* You didn't deny their number. You introduced a variable they hadn't considered, framed as curiosity rather than correction. The reader's reaction is "huh, that is interesting" rather than "here comes the lecture." Note: avoid the tempting "men commit 75% of crime" parallel. The counter is pre-loaded ("men can't be deported, immigration is a policy choice") and kills it in ten words. Use fresh parallels the opponent hasn't rehearsed against.

> **Example (Socratic variant)**
> 
> *Them:* "These people come from violent cultures, it's that simple."
> 
> *Disarm reply:* "Genuine question: if it's cultural, why do refugees from the same country show completely different crime rates depending on whether they're allowed to work? Same culture, different outcomes. What's the variable you'd point to?"
> 
> *Why this mode:* You didn't tell them they're wrong. You asked a question that has no comfortable answer within their framework. The reader watches them either engage (which forces them to grapple with the data) or dodge (which tells the reader everything).

### Stand with (support the target)

Not aimed at the attacker at all. Aimed at the person being attacked, or at the community watching someone get piled on. The [Dangerous Speech Project](https://dangerousspeech.org/considerations-for-successful-counterspeech/) documents solidarity as one of the most consistently effective responses to online hostility: it changes the social dynamic of the thread from "attacker vs. isolated target" to "attacker vs. community."

**When to use it:** Someone is being personally attacked, doxxed, or dogpiled. The attacker isn't worth engaging (pure insult, no falsifiable claim). A vulnerable person might be reading the thread and deciding whether it's safe to speak up.

> **Example**
> 
> *Them (to another user):* "Go back to your country if you don't like it here. Nobody asked you."
> 
> *Stand-with reply (to the person being attacked):* "I'm glad you're in this conversation. The data you shared earlier was useful and I've bookmarked it."
> 
> *Why this mode:* You didn't engage the attacker (they get nothing). You made the target visible as a valued participant. Every silent reader watching the exchange now sees that the community includes people who push back, not just people who attack. This changes who feels safe commenting next.

### How to choose

Ask four questions in order. Stop at the first "yes."

1. **Should I reply at all?** Does the comment have a falsifiable claim, enough visibility to mislead silent readers, and nothing in the thread already covers it? If any of those three fails: scroll past. This is the default, not the exception.
2. **Is someone being attacked personally?** If yes: stand with.
3. **Is the opponent engaging in good faith?** If yes: bridge. If clearly not: expose.
4. **Would humour or a question land better than a direct reply?** If the argument is absurd enough, or the thread is too heated for another serious reply: disarm.

When in doubt between bridge and expose, default to bridge. A bridge reply that was "too thorough" costs you nothing. An expose reply aimed at someone arguing honestly costs you the reader's trust.

If you notice you're always picking expose, that's a signal to check whether you've started treating every opponent as bad-faith (see "When you're the one who's wrong" below). If you notice you're replying to every comment in a thread, reread question 1.

### The ten rules for exposure mode

1. **3-5 lines max.** If the reply needs a paragraph to work, it doesn't work.
2. **No teacher voice.** Not "This is X because Y, source Z." Let their own words do the work.
3. **No concessions.** "You're right" kills exposure. Most readers stop there and think you lost.
4. **Statements as closers.** "Where's your source?" reads as uncertainty. "You have no source." reads as dominance.
5. **No source appendix.** Weave sources as drive-by facts mid-sentence. URLs at the bottom read as "please believe me."
6. **Match the opponent's energy.** Two-word comment gets a three-line reply. Never spend more effort than they did.
7. **Brevity IS the humiliation.** Short = barely tried. Long = they got under your skin.
8. **One angle per reply.** Three stacked arguments let the opponent pick the weakest. Hit once.
9. **Casual tone.** Coffee-sip delivery, not debate-club formatting.
10. **Wound topics as drive-by, not pivot.** "The party whose method you're parroting voted against child protection" works. A separate paragraph about child protection does not.

## What they do to counter you

Bad actors have their own playbook for defeating people who push back. Understanding their counter-strategies is as important as understanding their arguments. These patterns come from red-team analysis of how organised bad actors discuss countering progressive responders.

**They bait you into over-investing.** Their strongest tactic. A two-word provocation designed to trigger a five-paragraph reply. The reader sees someone who spent 30 minutes replying to "just deport them" and concludes you're obsessed. They post in ten threads in the time you spend on one. Defence: if your reply is more than three times the length of their comment, you've been baited. Shorten it.

**They strip your concessions.** When you write "the overrepresentation is real, but..." they screenshot the first half and drop the second. Your honesty becomes their headline. Defence: before any concession, read the sentence in isolation. If it works as an opposition headline, rephrase it. "The raw numbers show a gap, but raw numbers without context are misleading" is harder to strip than "the overrepresentation is real."

**They exploit your predictability.** Same study cited every thread, same structural argument, same concession-then-reframe. They've seen it enough to pre-load counters. Defence: rotate your sources and entry points. If you used the same study three threads in a row, use a different one. Same conclusion, different evidence.

**They play "reasonable" to win the middle.** "I'm not against immigration, I just think we need to look at the numbers honestly." Sounds moderate. Often is concern trolling. But the moderate framing wins readers because it doesn't trigger the "extremist" filter. Defence: don't call it concern trolling unless their comment history contradicts the moderate framing. If it does, name the inconsistency.

**They weaponise your academic tone.** Long study names and precise methodological language sound credible to you and out of touch to a normal reader. Common sense beats expertise in the court of public opinion, not because common sense is right, but because it's accessible. Defence: translate findings into plain language. Not "the study demonstrates a negative correlation between legal residence status and criminal burden" but "same people, allowed to work equals less crime, banned from working equals more crime."

**They switch to victim mode when losing.** "So I can't even ask questions anymore?" This reframes pushback as persecution, and it works when you've been even slightly aggressive. Any sarcasm, any condescension, any "educate yourself" hands them the frame. Defence: stay flat. Respond with substance only. The contrast between their complaint and your calm data is visible to every reader.

## The strongest weapon: their own source against them

Across hundreds of exchanges, this pattern outperformed everything else. The opponent can dismiss your source. They cannot dismiss their own.

**The half-read study.** An opponent quotes page 51 of a criminological study to support a cultural explanation. The study continues. The next pages attribute the differential to residence status and socioeconomic factors, not culture. "You stopped at page 51. The study goes on." His own study, his own argument turned inside out.

**The misread poll.** A poll is cited claiming "72% support" for an attack. Same poll: 90% of respondents didn't believe the reported atrocities occurred. Same respondents said attacks on civilians violate international law. Three numbers from the same poll, completely different picture of what "support" means.

**The boomerang statistic.** A media study used to claim "the media is covering this up." The study's actual finding: 94.6% of media-named suspects belonged to the minority group, against 34.3% of actual suspects. Three times overrepresented in coverage. "That's not a cover-up. That's the exact opposite."

**The unread questionnaire.** A user cited a study claiming "45% of Muslims are Islamism-affine." The study's own questionnaire included items like "Western sexual morality is depraved" (44.8% agreement), a morally conservative statement you'd find in any Evangelical survey, not an indicator of extremism. The manifest extremism category was 11.5%. Same study, completely different picture.

The formula: confirm the source is real, then show what else it says. The opponent did the citing. You just kept reading.

## The Inversion Audit

Before using any group comparison statistic, run three checks. This is non-negotiable, and it applies in both factual and exposure mode. I caught four own-goals in a single session. Each one would have handed the opponent a weapon they didn't have to find themselves.

**Invert the number.** If you cite "X% of crime by group A," compute what 100-X% means for group B relative to their population share. "38% of domestic homicides by the majority group" sounds like "the majority does it too." The inverse: 62% by a minority at roughly 15% of the population, proving massive overrepresentation. The opponent quotes your number and wins.

**Quote-back test.** Imagine the opponent pastes your exact sentence into their reply. Do they look smarter? If the answer is even "maybe," don't use the number.

**Per-capita trap.** Any absolute number comparing groups of different sizes is dangerous. Always check whether the per-capita calculation helps or hurts. If it hurts, find a different argument.

The most dangerous moment is when you find a number that perfectly supports your point. That's when confirmation bias is strongest and the inversion audit matters most.

## The overrepresentation rule

This principle applies whenever you're defending a group that is, statistically, overrepresented in some negative metric. Crime stats, welfare dependency, school exclusions, whatever the topic. Replies that deny or minimise a real statistical disparity lose credibility with every reader who can do basic arithmetic.

The correct move is acknowledgment followed by reframing, but the phrasing matters more than you think. "The overrepresentation is real" is a gift. Your opponent will screenshot it, strip the context, and quote it as vindication. Instead, build the context into the concession itself: "The suspect statistics overrepresent foreign nationals because the category includes offences only non-citizens can commit by definition." That version contains its own reframe and can't be cleanly split.

The policy fork must be visible to readers. "Culture as explanation means: keep them out. Living conditions as explanation means: faster legal processes, work permits, daily structure." Making the implication explicit turns an academic dispute into a political choice the reader can evaluate.

One gap to name honestly: the structural argument is strong for "what to do with people who are here." It is largely silent on "how many should come." When the opponent pivots to intake numbers, don't dodge and don't pretend your argument covers it. Name the gap: "That's a separate question from crime statistics, and conflating them is the move I won't make." The reader sees you chose not to answer, not that you missed it.

When facing a "reasonable mode" opponent (the parent worried about their daughter's walk home), lead with emotional acknowledgment before evidence. "Your fear is real. Here's what actually reduces risk in your neighbourhood" beats opening with a seven-year longitudinal study. The reader scared for their family needs to feel heard before they'll process data.

The most effective sub-point: stating both the favourable and unfavourable numbers. "Per-capita crime overall is declining. For sexual violence and robbery it's rising." Saying both is stronger than saying either one alone. It signals honesty, and honest pushback is the only kind that survives a stress-test.

## Fact-checking discipline

Precision builds trust. Imprecision destroys it.

**Use exact numbers.** 27,200 from the official report, not "roughly 28,000." The approximation invites the opponent to question your sources. The exact number signals you read the report.

**Never claim more than the source says.** "Comparatively rare" (from the study) is defensible. "Close to the average" (your gloss) is not. The difference is one reply away from credibility collapse. I used the wrong formulation twice, in two separate sessions, despite it being flagged the first time. Zombie claims resurface when the correction isn't specific enough.

**Verify before posting, not from memory.** Facts age. Your notes age. Verify the claim against current reality, not against your own files.

**When uncertain, cite toward the less favourable number.** If the real figure might be 1.2% or 1.8%, use 1.8%. Citing the number that's more favourable to your argument and getting caught understating by a third seeds doubt about everything else in the thread. Your strongest weapon (turning their source against them) depends entirely on your credibility. One wrong number destroys it.

**Stress-test, then read-test.** Stress-testing (hostile readers probing factual accuracy) catches wrong numbers. Read-testing (simulating how twenty silent readers perceive the tone) catches defensive framing, teacher voice, and effort mismatches. Both are needed. Wrong statistics, dead links, and misattributed years get caught by stress-tests. "Too long, too defensive" gets caught by read-tests.

## When to engage, when to skip, when to exit

**Always engage** when the comment has falsifiable claims, gets significant visibility, and silence would look like agreement.

**Never engage** when the comment is a pure insult, is buried in a thread nobody reads, or has already been dismantled by other users. A sixth reply on a dead argument makes you look like a pile-on.

**Engage with caution** when the opponent argues in good faith from an opposing position. Use factual mode. These are the most valuable conversations for readers.

**Exit after three rounds** without new arguments. Five exit types, depending on context:

- **Dignified exit.** "We're going in circles. Sources are above." Clean.
- **Summary exit.** "For everyone following along: [summary]." Frames the conclusion for the audience.
- **Tactic-exposing exit.** "The playbook: 1. Claim, 2. Ignore sources, 3. Repeat, 4. 'Define X'." Makes the method visible.
- **Savage exit.** "Okay Sergei, shift's over." Only when the opponent has clearly acted in bad faith.
- **Demobilisation counter.** When someone says "it's pointless anyway," that's disguised demobilisation, not genuine advice. "If it's pointless, why are you trying so hard to get me to stop?"

## When you're the one who's wrong

Cologne, New Year's Eve 2015/16. I watched progressive spaces (my spaces) suppress the ethnic dimension of mass sexual assaults because acknowledging it felt like handing ammunition to the right. Police delayed. Media hedged. The feminist response tried to hold anti-racism and anti-sexism together, which was correct, but the cover-up instinct had already done the damage. The far right didn't invent the vacuum. We created it by flinching.

I have no protocol for that. Neither does anyone else. The [Dangerous Speech Project](https://dangerousspeech.org/), GIFCT, the Future of Free Speech toolkit, none of them include an "I was wrong" step. The entire field assumes the practitioner's position is correct and the task is delivery. There is no tradition of updating based on what the opponent got right.

This matters because the typology above has a structural flaw. If every opponent maps to a named type (Barroom Expert, Parrot, Deflector), you've built the exact closed information loop you're fighting against. [C. Thi Nguyen's work on echo chambers](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/episteme/article/abs/echo-chambers-and-epistemic-bubbles/5D4AC3A808C538E17C50A7C09EC706F0) (Episteme, 2020) describes precisely this: a system where every outside voice gets pre-sorted into a distrust category. The typology is useful. It's also dangerous the moment it becomes a reflex.

Take the Selective Empath. I describe it as a bad actor type, someone who claims to care about victims but only discusses passports. That framing treats a position grounded in rules and consequences (borders matter, accountability matters) as pathology. Someone who focuses on structural prevention (me) sees passport talk as deflection. Someone who focuses on individual accountability sees prevention talk as deflection. Labelling one framing as a "type" is doing the same thing the article accuses opponents of: diagnosing the person instead of engaging the argument.

The honesty test is simple. When I write "the overrepresentation is real," would I follow that acknowledgment to any policy conclusion? If I'd acknowledge overrepresentation but would never support any policy that treats it as relevant, the acknowledgment is performative. The reader can smell that. A right-leaning moderate will quote "every reply is a performance for the silent readers" and conclude, correctly, that I care about winning, not about facts. [Hahn et al. (PNAS, 2024)](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2400930121) found that scientists who display genuine intellectual humility are perceived as more trustworthy across ideological lines. Performed humility has the opposite effect.

So: check whether the opponent is describing something real before you classify them. Run the inversion audit on your own position, not just your numbers. And if the answer is "I was wrong," say it in the thread, not in a private note six months later. One public correction builds more credibility with readers than forty sourced rebuttals.

## The meta-lesson

Pushing back against bad actors is not debate. Debate aims to convince the opponent. This aims to inoculate the audience. The opponent provides the material. Your reply is the sculpture the reader sees.

Accuracy matters more than rhetoric. One wrong number destroys ten good arguments. The most dangerous moment is when you find a number that perfectly supports your point. That's when you check it hardest.

The second most dangerous moment is when you've been right five times in a row. That's when you stop checking. The zombie claim resurfaces, the fact has aged, the inverse proves the other side's point, and you hand the opponent a weapon they never had to earn.

Check the inverse. Verify the source. Post.
