# Bot or Human? 30 Seconds Is All You Need

One in five social media posts is written by a bot. On X (formerly Twitter), 64% of accounts are flagged as potential bots. If you've ever spent twenty minutes crafting a careful reply to someone who turned out to be an automated account, you already know why this matters.

Most people waste their best arguments on accounts that aren't even human. Here's how to stop doing that.

## The 30-second check

You don't need a forensics degree. You need five signals. Check the profile before you type a single word.

**1. Profile picture.** Generic stock photo? AI-generated face? No picture at all? Red flag. AI-generated faces have a tell: they're almost too symmetrical, and the background is often blurred or incoherent. If the person looks like a LinkedIn headshot but the account posts exclusively about immigration policy, something is off.

**2. Username.** Strings of random numbers (user38472918) are a classic bot signature. Legitimate accounts occasionally have numbers, but "patriot_truth_8847291" is not a person choosing a clever handle. It's an account factory.

**3. Follower ratio.** Following 10,000 accounts with fewer than 200 followers is the bot equivalent of handing out flyers in a crowd. Real humans accumulate followers roughly in proportion to how much they post. Bots broadcast. They don't attract.

**4. Posting frequency.** Humans sleep. Bots don't. If an account posts 50 or more times per day, across multiple time zones, with no gaps, you're not dealing with an insomniac. You're dealing with software. Check the timestamps. Consistent posting at 3am, 7am, 11am, and 4pm across a 24-hour cycle is a machine pattern.

**5. Conversation behaviour.** This is the most reliable signal. Bots broadcast; they don't converse. They reply to trending topics with pre-loaded talking points. They never adjust to what you actually said. They use the same hashtags in every post. A human who disagrees with you will engage with your specific argument. A bot will paste a paragraph that could be a response to any comment in the thread.

Three or more of these signals? Probably a bot. Move on.

## The quick-reference checklist

- Generic or AI-generated profile picture
- Username is a number soup
- Follows thousands, followed by almost no one
- Posts more than 50 times per day
- Never responds to the actual content of replies
- Only reposts, no original content
- Replies within seconds (consistently under one minute)

Three hits: probably a bot. Don't feed it.

## Troll or true believer?

Not every bad-faith account is automated. Some are human, and the distinction changes your strategy.

A troll switches positions constantly. They don't care about the argument; they care about your reaction. When you get angry, they're winning. The tell: they seem to enjoy your frustration. They'll abandon a claim the moment you refute it and pivot to something entirely different, not because they're convinced but because the old line stopped getting a rise.

A true believer stays consistent. They defend the same position with increasing emotion. When you present evidence, they don't pivot. They dig in. They're frustrated that you won't see what seems obvious to them.

Why this matters: trolls get the exit. One calm, sourced reply for the benefit of anyone reading, then you leave. True believers (occasionally) get the conversation. Not a lecture, not a fact barrage, but questions. "How did you come to that conclusion?" "What would change your mind?"

The research supports this distinction. Deep canvassing studies (Science, 2016) found that a single 10-minute empathetic conversation can reduce prejudice for at least three months. But that only works with people who hold genuine beliefs and have room for doubt. It doesn't work on someone whose goal is to waste your afternoon.

## The audience that matters

Here's the part most people miss. You're not writing for the person you're arguing with. You're writing for the hundred people reading silently.

The screamer in the comments will never change their mind during a public thread. The lurker, though, is watching how both sides handle themselves. They notice who stays calm. They notice who cites sources. They notice who resorts to insults when the argument gets tight.

Every reply you write is a performance for an audience you can't see. The Center for Countering Digital Hate puts it directly: ignoring trolls doesn't work when the troll is spreading disinformation. Silence reads as acceptance to the people watching. But engaging doesn't mean arguing. It means one clean, factual reply, then out.

Short. Sourced. Dignified. Then leave.

## The SIFT method (for when the content looks real)

Sometimes the account is human but the claim is garbage. Before you respond to "Study proves X!", run four steps. Professional fact-checkers spend fewer than 30 seconds on the original source before opening new tabs.

**Stop.** Don't react immediately. Do you know this source?

**Investigate the source.** Google the author, the publication, the organisation. Who funds them? What's their track record?

**Find better coverage.** Search for the same claim from other outlets. If only one source is reporting it, that's a signal.

**Trace the original.** Go upstream. Does the study actually say what the headline claims? Often the abstract says one thing and the tweet quoting it says something quite different.

This takes 30 seconds with practice. It saves you from amplifying a claim that falls apart on contact with the primary source.

## What this looks like in practice

Someone posts a provocative claim in a thread you care about. Before typing:

1. Check the profile (30 seconds). Bot? Close the tab.
2. Troll or believer? (Read their last five comments.) Troll? One reply for the audience, then out.
3. Genuine person with a bad source? SIFT the claim. Reply with what the source actually says.

Most threads don't deserve your afternoon. The ones that do are the ones where a lurker is quietly forming an opinion. That's where your calm, sourced, three-sentence reply does more than any 500-word rebuttal.

Write for the hundred silent readers. Not the one screamer.
