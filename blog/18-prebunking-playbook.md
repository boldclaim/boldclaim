# The Prebunking Playbook: How to Inoculate Against Disinformation Before It Arrives

Debunking is a losing game. By the time you've fact-checked a false claim, it's already been shared 10,000 times, embedded in people's mental models, and reinforced by repetition. The correction always travels slower than the lie.

Prebunking flips the sequence. Instead of chasing false claims after they spread, you warn people about manipulation techniques before they encounter them. The research says this works significantly better, and it takes about five minutes.

## The Science: Inoculation Theory

The concept comes from medical immunology, applied to information. Expose someone to a weakened form of a manipulation technique, explain how it works, and their resistance to the real thing increases measurably.

A Harvard meta-analysis (Misinformation Review, 2023) across 37,075 participants found that prebunking improves people's ability to distinguish reliable from unreliable information, without making them generally distrustful. That second part matters. Prebunking doesn't create cynics who believe nothing. It creates readers who spot the technique.

## What You're Inoculating Against

Prebunking doesn't target specific claims ("the AfD didn't really vote against the minimum wage"). It targets techniques. The five most common:

**Emotional language.** Headlines designed to trigger outrage before you've read the article. "SHOCKING: Government BETRAYS citizens." The capitalisation, the loaded verb, the vagueness. Once you've been shown this pattern, you start noticing it everywhere.

**False authority.** "A study proves..." without naming the study, the journal, or the methodology. Or citing a real scientist making claims outside their field. A Nobel physicist opining on immigration policy is not an authority on immigration policy.

**Scapegoating.** Complex problem, simple villain. Economic anxiety becomes "the immigrants." Housing shortage becomes "the refugees." A named enemy is more satisfying than a structural explanation. Recognising the pattern means asking: does this explanation account for the actual complexity, or has it been shaved down to fit a bumper sticker?

**Cherry-picking.** One statistic, stripped of context. "Foreign nationals are 35% of crime suspects!" True, but suspicion rates include tourist crime, visa violations, and offences citizens can't commit by definition. The conviction rate tells a different story. Cherry-picking survives only as long as the audience doesn't ask "what's the full picture?"

**False equivalence.** "Both sides are the same." Equating a party that has been classified as a confirmed extremist threat by the domestic intelligence service with parties that have policy disagreements. The technique flattens all distinctions so that nothing matters and nobody is worse than anyone else.

## The Five-Minute Inoculation

You don't need a classroom. You need a conversation before the disinformation arrives.

**Before a family gathering:** "I've been reading about how fake news works. Apparently the main trick isn't lying outright. It's using real numbers without context. Like, they'll post one statistic that sounds terrifying, but if you look at the full dataset, it says the opposite. Just something to watch for."

That's it. No lecture. No political position. Just a heads-up about a technique. The person you're talking to now has a filter installed. When they encounter the technique in the wild, something clicks: "wait, someone told me about this."

**Before an election:** "There's a game called Bad News (getbadnews.com) where you play as a disinformation creator. Takes fifteen minutes. It's weirdly fun, and afterwards you spot the tricks in real posts. Might be worth a go."

**In a group chat:** When someone shares a suspicious article, don't fact-check it (yet). Instead: "Before we get into whether this is true, notice the technique. The headline uses emotional language, the source isn't named, and the statistic has no context. That's three red flags before we even check the claim."

You've just taught lateral reading without calling it that. The group now has a shared vocabulary for spotting manipulation.

## Why Prebunking Beats Debunking

Debunking has a structural problem called the continued influence effect. Even after a correction, the original false claim persists in memory. People remember the claim but forget the retraction.

Prebunking avoids this because there's no false claim to remember. You're not saying "claim X is wrong." You're saying "here's how manipulation works." The person never encodes the specific lie, so there's nothing to retract.

Prebunking also scales. You can't debunk every individual false claim (there are too many, they mutate daily). But you can teach five manipulation techniques, and those five cover roughly 90% of online disinformation. The techniques don't change. Only the topics do.

## The Limits

Prebunking works best on people who haven't yet committed to a position. If someone has spent two years in a Telegram group consuming a specific narrative, a five-minute conversation about manipulation techniques won't undo that.

It also requires trust. A stranger telling you "watch out for emotional language" sounds patronising. A friend or family member saying the same thing sounds like genuine concern. Who delivers the inoculation matters as much as what they say.

And prebunking fades. The Harvard meta-analysis found the effect weakens over time without reinforcement. A single conversation helps. Regular conversations help more. This is not a one-shot solution. It's a practice.

## Start Small

You don't need to become a media literacy instructor. You need to do one thing: the next time someone you know shares something that uses one of the five techniques above, name the technique. Not the claim. The technique.

"That's classic cherry-picking, one number without context" is more useful than "that's not true, here's the real number." The first teaches a skill. The second wins one argument.

Prebunking is slower than debunking and less dramatic. But it works on the only timeline that matters: the one where fewer people fall for the next lie, and the one after that.
