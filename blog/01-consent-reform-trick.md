# The Consent Reform Trick

Refugees arrived in Germany in 2015. Sexual offence statistics "exploded" in 2017. The far right treats this as an open-and-shut case. But there is a two-year gap in their story, and the thing that fills it is not migration. It is a law.

On 10 November 2016, Germany reformed its sexual criminal law. The reform, known as "Nein heisst Nein" (no means no), was the most significant overhaul of Section 177 of the Strafgesetzbuch in decades. It did three things that directly changed the statistics.

First, sexual acts against someone's recognisably opposing will became a crime. Before the reform, prosecutors had to prove the perpetrator used physical force or threats. A victim who froze, who said no but was not physically overpowered, had no legal recourse. The new law closed that gap. Second, sexual harassment (Section 184i) became a criminal offence for the first time. Not reclassified, not upgraded. It simply did not exist as a category before November 2016. Third, group sexual assaults got their own offence category (Section 184j), a direct response to the Cologne New Year's Eve attacks of 2015/16. "Antanzen" (the practice of surrounding and groping women in crowds) became prosecutable.

So when the 2017 police crime statistics landed and showed a spike in sexual offences, the explanation was straightforward. You cannot record crimes that do not legally exist yet. The moment they become crimes, they appear in the statistics. This is not a mystery. It is arithmetic.

## The timeline that breaks the narrative

Lay out the dates and the far-right story collapses:

- **2015**: Refugees arrive in large numbers.
- **November 2016**: Sexual criminal law reformed. New offences created.
- **2017**: Statistics rise.

If the spike were caused by refugees, it would have shown up in 2016 at the latest, not two years after their arrival. The statistical jump tracks perfectly with the date the law changed. Prof. Dr. Tatjana Hoernle, writing in KriPoZ in 2018, confirmed what the timeline already tells you: the increase in recorded Section 177 offences is explained by the expanded scope of criminal liability, not necessarily by more actual assaults.

Correctiv's fact-check in November 2025 reached the same conclusion: "The increase in suspects is only of limited significance, since there were tightening measures and changes in criminal law."

## A feminist victory, weaponised

This is where it gets particularly cynical. The Nein-heisst-Nein reform was a feminist achievement. Women's rights organisations had campaigned for years to close the gaps in German sexual criminal law. The Istanbul Convention demanded it. The Cologne attacks accelerated it. When the Bundestag finally passed the reform in July 2016, it was celebrated across the political spectrum as long overdue.

The far right then took the statistical effect of that reform and repackaged it as evidence of immigrant danger. They weaponised a law designed to protect women into propaganda against refugees. The people who voted against strengthening women's rights (the AfD opposed the reform) now cite the resulting statistics as proof that foreigners are a threat.

## What the actual data says

The ifo Institute published a study in February 2025 analysing police crime statistics across all German districts from 2018 to 2023. Their finding: no statistical correlation between the share of foreigners in a district and the local crime rate. This holds for homicides and sexual offences specifically. It also holds when looking specifically at refugees.

Germany's homicide rate has roughly halved since 1995 (from around 1.5 to 0.9 per 100,000). The country is one of the safest in the world, with a murder rate 6.4 times lower than the United States and 8.7 times lower than Russia.

None of this means that every individual crime statistic is unproblematic. But the macro story, the one the far right tells of a country made dangerous by migration, is contradicted by the government's own numbers.

## The pattern to watch for

Whenever someone cites a statistical jump, ask what else changed that year. New offences, new reporting requirements, new counting methods: these are the boring explanations that break dramatic narratives. The 2017 sexual offence spike is a textbook case. The law changed. The numbers followed. Refugees had nothing to do with it.

The far right knows this. They cite the statistic anyway, because the two-year gap between 2015 and 2017 is just wide enough that most people will not bother to check what happened in between.
