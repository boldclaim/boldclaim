# Solar Is 41% Cheaper and Nobody Noticed

The argument against renewables used to have two legs: too expensive and too unreliable. Both are now factually inverted, and the data is not ambiguous. IRENA's 2025 report found that solar is 41% cheaper than the cheapest fossil fuel alternative. Onshore wind is 53% cheaper. 91% of all new renewable energy projects commissioned in 2024 came in below the cost of any fossil option.

Globally, renewables saved $467 billion in fossil fuel costs in 2024 alone. This is not a projection. It already happened.

## The cost curve that ended the argument

Solar electricity has dropped 90% in cost since 2010. Wind has dropped 70%. These are not incremental improvements. They are the kind of cost curves that, in any other industry, would be treated as a revolution.

The "too expensive" talking point made sense fifteen years ago. Repeating it now requires actively ignoring the price tags. New solar and wind installations are cheaper than new coal or gas plants in virtually every market on earth. Not because of subsidies (though those helped accelerate the early phases), but because the technology matured, manufacturing scaled, and the learning curves did what learning curves do.

The trend is also irreversible in the economic sense. Once the capital is deployed and the panels are on rooftops, the marginal cost of solar electricity is near zero. There is no fuel to buy. Fossil plants, by contrast, are permanently exposed to commodity price swings. The 2022 gas crisis taught Europe that lesson at a cost of hundreds of billions.

## The Spain blackout that proved the opposite

When Spain experienced its major blackout in April 2025, the instant narrative was predictable: renewables failed. It spread fast, and it was wrong.

The Spanish government's official investigation report (June 2025) and the report from grid operator Red Electrica both reached the same conclusion. The blackout was caused by the failure of conventional power plants to maintain voltage regulation. Not renewables.

Grid Strategies, an independent energy consultancy, reviewed both reports and summarised: "Renewable generators were not the cause of the blackout. Both reports recommend that renewable and battery resources be a primary solution to prevent such events in the future."

The blackout was not a case of renewables failing. It was a case of the old grid infrastructure failing to keep up. The recommended fix was more renewables, not fewer.

## The "Dunkelflaute" red herring

"What about when the wind doesn't blow and the sun doesn't shine?" This question gets posed as though nobody in the energy sector has considered it. They have. The answer is not mysterious.

Batteries handle daily fluctuations. They are not designed to bridge a two-week windless, overcast spell (a "Dunkelflaute"), and nobody serious claims otherwise. For those rare events, gas-fired backup plants fill the gap. Germany is building 12 GW of gas capacity for exactly this purpose, hydrogen-ready and scheduled to be climate-neutral by 2045.

The crucial distinction: a gas plant running a few hundred hours per year as backup is not the same as a gas plant running as baseload. One makes you independent. The other, at 77% fossil primary energy and $113 oil, keeps you dependent. The Dunkelflaute argument conflates the two, either out of ignorance or on purpose.

## The political inversion

The AfD's energy platform calls for continued coal subsidies, a halt to wind turbine construction, and withdrawal from the Paris Agreement. This was already questionable policy in 2020. In 2025, with solar at 41% below fossil parity and renewables overtaking coal in global electricity generation for the first time, it is simply an invitation to fall behind.

Germany's competitors are not debating whether renewables work. China deployed more solar capacity in 2024 than the rest of the world combined. The United States, despite its political fractures, added record renewable capacity. The market has made its decision. The only question is whether Germany participates in the upside or pays for the transition as a customer rather than a producer.

The "too expensive, too unreliable" framing is now the energy equivalent of arguing that smartphones are a fad. The numbers moved. The argument did not.
