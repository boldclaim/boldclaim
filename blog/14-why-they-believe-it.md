# Why They Believe It: The Psychology Behind Disinformation

The most common mistake in counter-speech is assuming that the person you are arguing with is stupid. They are almost certainly not. The human brain is a pattern-matching machine that evolved to make fast decisions in dangerous environments, not to evaluate statistical methodology. Disinformation works because it exploits the same cognitive shortcuts that keep us alive.

Understanding why intelligent people believe wrong things changes how you talk to them. It also explains why fact-bombing (the instinct to hurl data at someone until they surrender) almost never works.

## The machinery of belief

Five cognitive biases do most of the heavy lifting for disinformation. They are not flaws. They are features of human cognition that happen to be exploitable.

**Confirmation bias** is the engine. People seek, notice, and remember information that confirms what they already believe. Someone who is anxious about immigration will notice every article about a migrant crime and mentally file it as evidence. The thousand uneventful days do not register because they do not fit the pattern the brain is tracking.

This is not laziness. It is efficiency. The brain is managing an overwhelming volume of information by prioritising what seems relevant. The problem is that "relevant" means "consistent with existing beliefs," not "statistically representative." Everyone does this. You do it. I do it. The question is not whether we are biased, but whether we have systems for catching our own bias.

**The availability heuristic** is the amplifier. People estimate how common something is based on how easily they can recall examples. Ten dramatic news articles about migrant crime create a sense that migrant crime is everywhere, even when the per-capita rate is falling. The brain confuses "easy to remember" with "frequently occurring."

Media structure makes this worse. Crime reporting systematically overrepresents migrants as suspects (the Hestermann study found a factor of 3x). The availability heuristic takes that skewed media diet and converts it into a skewed sense of reality. The person is not lying when they say "it feels like crime is exploding." It does feel that way. The feeling is wrong, but the feeling is real.

**The illusory truth effect** is the cement. Repeat a claim often enough and people start to believe it, regardless of whether it is true. This has been demonstrated in laboratory conditions: statements rated as more likely to be true after three exposures than after one, even when participants were told the statements might be false.

This is why disinformation campaigns focus on repetition rather than evidence. "The Great Replacement" does not need to be proven. It needs to be said a thousand times. Each repetition makes it feel slightly more plausible, slightly more like something "everyone knows." By the time someone encounters a debunking, the claim has been cemented by sheer familiarity.

**The Dunning-Kruger effect** is the confidence generator. People with limited knowledge in a domain tend to overestimate their competence in that domain. Someone who has read three Telegram posts about vaccination schedules genuinely believes they have done "their own research" and can evaluate immunological evidence. Someone who has watched a YouTube video about crime statistics genuinely believes they understand criminological methodology.

This is not arrogance. It is a calibration problem. You need expertise to know what expertise looks like. A first-year medical student does not know enough to understand how much they do not know. A seasoned clinician does. The Dunning-Kruger effect means that the people most confidently wrong are the ones least equipped to recognise their own errors.

**The backfire effect** is the trap. Sometimes, presenting evidence against a belief makes the belief stronger. The person feels attacked (because their belief has become part of their identity), and the emotional response of defending their identity overwhelms the cognitive process of evaluating evidence.

This is the one that makes counter-speech so difficult. You show someone that their claim is factually wrong. They do not update. They dig in. You show more evidence. They dig in further. The conversation feels like pushing against a wall that gets stronger the harder you push.

## Why this matters for how you talk to people

If you understand these five mechanisms, several things follow.

**Fact-bombing does not work.** Dumping ten sources on someone whose belief is identity-level will trigger the backfire effect, not persuasion. The evidence does not reach the part of the brain that updates beliefs. It reaches the part that defends identity. You are not arguing with their logic. You are arguing with their sense of self.

**Repetition is their strongest weapon, not evidence.** The illusory truth effect means that a simple, repeated lie will outperform a complex, one-time correction. If you want to counter a false narrative, you need to repeat the correction as often as the narrative is repeated. One debunking in one thread does not undo a thousand repetitions across a thousand threads.

**Emotional validation comes before factual correction.** The backfire effect activates when people feel attacked. If you start with "you are wrong," the drawbridge goes up. If you start with "I understand why that feels concerning," the drawbridge stays down long enough for information to cross. This is not being soft. It is being strategic.

**Questions outperform statements.** Street epistemology (the Socratic method adapted for everyday conversations) works because it routes around the backfire effect. Instead of telling someone they are wrong, you ask them how they arrived at their belief. "How confident are you, on a scale of 1 to 10?" "What would it take to change your mind?" "How do you know your source is reliable?" These questions do not trigger identity defence. They trigger self-reflection. The person argues with themselves, which is the only argument they might lose.

**The reachable and the unreachable are different populations.** Some people are genuinely uncertain. They are asking real questions, showing genuine doubt, responding to new information. These are the people worth investing time in. Other people have fused their beliefs with their identity so completely that no amount of evidence or empathy will move them. Recognising the difference early saves hours.

## Signs someone is reachable

They ask real questions ("but where does that number come from?"). They show uncertainty ("I am not sure, but I read that..."). They engage with your sources, even critically. They bring new arguments rather than repeating the same talking points. They stay respectful even when disagreeing.

## Signs someone is not reachable

They repeat the same claim for the third time without acknowledging your response. They reject every source as biased, fake, or compromised. They attack you personally instead of engaging your argument. Their belief is clearly part of their group identity. They use the phrase "do your own research" as a conversation-ender.

## The seed, not the harvest

A single online exchange will not change a mind. That is not what you are doing. You are planting a seed of doubt. Maybe it grows. Maybe it does not. The Nantes study found that 58% of former cult members said a conversation with a friend or family member was the trigger for their exit. But only when the person already had small doubts.

You cannot force someone to doubt. You can make doubting feel safe. You can show that questioning a belief does not require abandoning an identity. You can model the behaviour of someone who holds strong positions and still updates when the evidence changes.

The psychology of disinformation is not a reason to give up. It is a reason to change tactics. Stop trying to win arguments. Start trying to plant questions.
