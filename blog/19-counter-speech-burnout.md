# Counter-Speech Burnout: How to Fight Without Destroying Yourself

There's a specific kind of exhaustion that comes from arguing with strangers on the internet about whether the Holocaust memorial is a "monument of shame" or whether crime statistics mean what someone claims they mean. It's not the same as work burnout. It's more like being a goalkeeper who faces penalties all day, every day, knowing that the other team has an infinite supply of balls.

If you do counter-speech regularly, you will burn out. Not if. When. The question is whether you recognise it in time and whether you've built the structures to survive it.

## What Burnout Looks Like

The physical signs are obvious once you know them. Heart racing when you see a notification. Jaw clenching while reading comments. Lying in bed composing the reply you should have written. Checking your phone during conversations with actual humans to see if someone responded to your thread.

The mental signs are subtler and more dangerous. The compulsion to reply ("I MUST answer this"). The inability to let a wrong comment stand without correction. Anger that doesn't dissipate after you close the app. The growing sense that you're fighting alone against a tide that never stops.

The social signs are the ones your friends notice before you do. Every conversation turns political. People start avoiding certain topics around you. You're physically present but mentally still in the thread from three hours ago.

If you recognise three or more of these, you're not "passionate." You're burning out.

## Why Counter-Speech Burns Faster Than Other Activism

Marching in a protest has a start time and an end time. Volunteering at a food bank has a shift. Counter-speech has neither. The internet never closes. There is always another thread, another false claim, another person confidently posting something that is demonstrably wrong. The supply of disinformation is literally infinite, generated by bots, coordinated campaigns, and ordinary people who genuinely believe what they're sharing.

You are one person with finite energy fighting an infinite supply of nonsense. The maths doesn't work, and pretending it does is how you end up burned out, radicalised, and no use to anyone. Add constant exposure to dehumanising language, racist rhetoric, and casual cruelty, and you have a recipe for emotional exhaustion that accumulates faster than you notice.

## The Boundaries That Actually Work

**Time limits.** Thirty minutes per day for political discussions online. Set an actual timer. When it goes off, stop. Mid-sentence if necessary. The thread will be there tomorrow. Your mental health might not be.

**Thread limits.** Maximum five replies per thread. After five exchanges, you've said everything worth saying. Rounds six through fifteen are for your ego, not for the Mitleser. Exit with a summary and a source link. Walk away.

**Time-of-day limits.** No political discussions after 10pm. You are tired, emotional, less precise, and more likely to write something you'll regret. The intersection of "exhausted" and "someone is wrong on the internet" produces nothing good.

**Topic rotation.** Don't spend every session on the same subject. If you spent Monday on immigration statistics, spend Tuesday on climate disinformation or economic policy. Variety isn't just intellectually healthy. It prevents the specific despair that comes from seeing the same debunked claim resurface for the twentieth time.

## The Reframe That Saves You

"You are not responsible for every thread."

Read it again. Let it settle.

You are not the last line of defence against disinformation. You are one person contributing to a larger effort. If you skip a thread, someone else might pick it up. If nobody picks it up, the world does not end. The Mitleser who reads an unchallenged false claim today will encounter a correction somewhere else tomorrow.

The maths of counter-speech is not "I must answer everything." It's "one good reply is worth more than ten exhausted ones." Quality degrades with fatigue. Your fifth reply of the evening is worse than your first, and by the tenth you're probably doing more harm than good (snapping at people, making factual errors, sounding unhinged to the silent readers you're trying to reach).

Taking a break is not surrender. It's rotation.

## When to Take a Break

**Immediately** if you notice the physical signs (racing heart, insomnia about threads, appetite changes).

**This week** if you notice the mental signs (compulsive checking, persistent anger, the feeling of fighting alone).

**Today** if you notice the social signs (friends avoiding topics, real-life conversations hijacked by online arguments).

A break means: no political social media for a defined period. Not "I'll just lurk." Not "I'll read but not reply." Off. Completely. The threads will survive without you. The disinformation will still be there when you come back. But you'll come back sharper, calmer, and more effective than the depleted version of yourself that's currently rage-typing at midnight.

## How to Come Back

After a break (a day, a week, a month), re-enter gradually. One thread per day. Timer set. Exit strategy planned before you start writing. Pick your battles deliberately instead of reacting to whatever crosses your feed first.

And redefine what success looks like. It's not "I won the argument." It's not "I changed their mind." It's "I left one calm, sourced reply for the Mitleser." That's the whole job. One reply. Then out.

The marathon is not won by the sprinter. It's won by the person who's still running a year from now. Burnout is not a badge of honour. It's a strategy failure. Protect the resource that matters most (your capacity to keep going), and you'll do more good over a decade than any single heroic thread ever could.

Activism that destroys you helps no one. Rest is not retreat. It's maintenance.
