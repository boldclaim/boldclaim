# Germany Is Twice as Safe as 1995

Germany's homicide rate has nearly halved since 1995. In that year, the rate sat at roughly 1.5 to 2.5 per 100,000 (including post-reunification ZERV cases). By 2024, it was around 0.9. The country got safer, steadily and measurably, across three decades that included the largest migration wave in its postwar history.

Nobody in 1995 was afraid to walk the streets. The numbers were worse then. The fear is louder now.

## The ifo study that should have ended the debate

In February 2025, the ifo Institut published a study by Alipour and Adema analysing police crime statistics (PKS) across all German districts from 2018 to 2023. The finding: zero statistical correlation between the share of foreigners in a district and that district's crime rate. Not for crime overall. Not for homicides. Not for sexual offences. Not specifically for refugees.

The quote from lead researcher Dr Jean-Victor Alipour: "We find no correlation between a rising share of foreigners in a district and the local crime rate."

This is not an activist think tank. The ifo is Germany's most-cited economic research institute. Their method was straightforward: take the actual data, control for the relevant variables, see what correlates. Foreign population share does not predict local crime.

The overrepresentation of non-Germans in raw crime statistics has a simpler explanation. Foreigners in Germany are disproportionately concentrated in urban areas, where crime rates are higher for everyone. Young, male, poor populations commit more crime regardless of passport. When the KFN (Criminological Research Institute of Lower Saxony) controlled for residence status, they found that recognised refugees with secure legal status showed significantly lower crime rates than those stuck in limbo on temporary toleration permits. The gradient runs along legal status, not origin.

## The media magnifying glass

If the statistics don't support the narrative, something else must be generating the perception. Thomas Hestermann's 2025 study for the Mediendienst Integration measured exactly what.

When German TV news names a crime suspect's origin, 94.6% of the time that suspect is non-German. The actual share of non-German suspects in police statistics: 34.3%. Suspects from Muslim-majority countries are represented at 70.3% in TV coverage, versus 15.8% in the PKS. That is roughly four times their actual share.

This was not limited to tabloid outlets. Hestermann found no significant difference between conservative media (Die Welt) and left-leaning outlets (taz). The bias is structural, not ideological. A crime with an Afghan suspect generates roughly twice the media coverage of a comparable crime with a German suspect. Same act, different amplification.

Criminologist Gina Wollinger of the NRW Police Academy calls this the "migrantisation" of crime. The risk factor is not origin. It is poverty and exposure to violence. But poverty does not make headlines.

## The international context nobody mentions

Germany's homicide rate of 0.9 per 100,000 makes it one of the safest countries on the planet. For comparison: the United States sits at 5.76 (6.4 times higher), Russia at 7.87 (8.7 times higher), and the global average is around 5.8. The US alone averages roughly 160 school shooting incidents per year (Everytown, 2024). A single bad month in American schools would overwhelm Germany's annual homicide statistics.

This context never appears in the debate. The framing is always relative to an imagined safer past, never relative to the actual world.

## The perception gap is the product

The PKS records investigations, not convictions. It includes immigration-specific offences (illegal entry, residency violations) that only non-Germans can commit by definition. Victimisation surveys show that the same assault is reported to police at a rate of 6.6% when both parties are German, but 12% when the suspect is perceived as non-German. Same act, nearly double the reporting rate.

Every layer of the pipeline (what gets reported, what gets investigated, what gets covered, what gets shared) amplifies non-German suspects. The result is a perception of danger that the underlying data does not support.

Germany in 2024 is measurably, statistically, almost twice as safe as in 1995. The people telling you otherwise are not citing numbers. They are citing feelings, manufactured at scale.
