# Their Playbook: How Right-Wing Accounts Systematically Neutralise Online Opposition

*Written from their perspective. Not because I agree, but because understanding how they think about us makes us harder to beat.*

I spent six months watching how right-wing accounts operate on German Reddit. Not what they say (that part is obvious), but how they categorise the people pushing back against them, which counter-moves they fear, and which ones they've learned to neutralise. What follows is their playbook, reconstructed from their perspective. The voice is theirs. The analysis is mine. A threat model only works when you see yourself through the opponent's eyes.

## The Types

Not everyone pushing back is the same. Treating every counter-speech reply identically is how you lose threads you should win. Here are the types.

### The Professor

Long replies. Multiple sources. Structured arguments with concessions built in ("the overrepresentation is real, but..."). Usually writes more than anyone else in the thread. Sounds authoritative. Often is.

**Why they're dangerous.** They look credible to lurkers. A well-sourced reply with a concession at the top reads as honest, and readers trust honest-sounding people.

**Why they're beatable.** They can't stop writing. A Professor who drops a six-paragraph reply to your two-line comment has already lost the energy war. They look obsessed. You look unbothered. Their length is their weakness because most readers won't finish it. The ones who do were already on their side.

**How to beat them.** Don't engage the substance. Pick the one concession they made ("so you admit the overrepresentation is real") and quote it back without the reframing that followed. Their own words, stripped of context, become your best ammunition. They gave you the concession to sound honest. You use it to sound vindicated.

### The Gotcha Artist

Waits for you to get one number slightly wrong, then builds their entire reply around the error. Ignores your actual argument.

**Why they're dangerous.** One wrong number really does hurt. Lurkers remember "that person got the statistic wrong" longer than they remember ten correct statistics.

**Why they're beatable.** They're so hungry for the gotcha that they ignore the argument. "You found a typo. Congratulations. Now address the point."

**How to beat them.** Be accurate enough to deny them the opening. When they do catch one, correct it casually ("fair enough, 27,200 not 28,000, the point stands") and move on. Doubling down on a wrong number is the worst response. Apologising at length is the second worst.

### The Moral Superior

Frames everything as ethics. "This is about human dignity." "This is how it started in the 1930s." Their weapon is making you feel like a bad person for stating facts.

**Why they're dangerous.** The 1930s comparison works on German audiences specifically. Nobody wants to be on the wrong side of that analogy.

**Why they're beatable.** By turning a factual discussion into a moral one, they've abandoned the terrain where evidence matters. Make the moral framing look like a dodge: "I cited a statistic. You compared me to a Nazi. One of us is avoiding the conversation."

**How to beat them.** Stay factual. Every time they escalate to morality, pull back to numbers. The reader sees someone stating facts and someone having a meltdown about it.

### The Tactic Namer

They don't argue your point. They name your "technique." "That's a gish gallop." "Classic motte-and-bailey." "You're sealioning." The Wikipedia page on rhetorical fallacies, deployed as a cheat code.

**Why they're dangerous.** Naming the tactic works. Once a lurker reads "that's called sealioning" and clicks the link, the label replaces the argument.

**Why they're beatable.** They've substituted taxonomy for thinking. "That's a gish gallop" describes the shape of your argument without rebutting a single point. "You named a fallacy. You didn't answer the question. Those are different things."

**How to beat them.** Ask a direct question after they name the tactic. "You called it sealioning. Fine. Now answer: what's the per-capita rate?" They either answer (and you're back on your terrain) or dodge (and the lurker sees the label was the whole act).

### The Source Dumper

Drops three to five links at the bottom of their reply. Rarely quotes from them. The links do the arguing.

**Why they're dangerous.** Links look like evidence. Most readers won't click them, but the presence of links creates an impression of rigour.

**Why they're beatable.** Click the links. About a third of the time the source doesn't support their claim or contradicts it. "Your second link actually says the opposite of what you claimed on page 12" is devastating. They weren't expecting anyone to check.

**How to beat them.** "Five links, zero quotes. Which one makes your argument? Pick one and I'll engage it." Forces them to commit to a single source, which is much easier to handle than a wall of URLs.

### The Bot Accuser

Dismisses any organised or rapid response as "coordinated" or "astroturfed." Three people disagreeing must be a brigade. New account equals sockpuppet.

**Why they're dangerous.** In spaces where bot activity is real, the accusation has surface plausibility.

**Why they're beatable.** Unfalsifiable accusations are also unpersuasive to lurkers who think for themselves. Someone who says "you're all bots" looks paranoid, not perceptive. Lean into the absurdity: "You're right, the entire internet is a bot except you. Now about the statistic you didn't address..."

### The Ally Rallier

Doesn't argue with you. Replies to the person you were talking to with "don't let them silence you." Reframes the thread from a debate into a solidarity exercise.

**Why they're dangerous.** They change the emotional register. Once a conversation becomes about "support" rather than "facts," you've lost the frame.

**Why they're beatable.** They've abandoned the argument entirely. Ignore them completely. Engaging makes you look like you're attacking a bystander. Stay locked on the person who made the original claim.

## Their Moves

### The Source Boomerang

Their best weapon. They take your source, read further than you quoted, and use the rest against you. If you cited page 51, they read page 52. If you quoted one finding, they quote three others from the same study.

**How to survive it.** Stop citing studies you haven't read completely. If you're going to use a source, know every page. If you can't commit to that, don't cite it. They're better at the citation game because they spend more time on individual threads.

### The Concession Trap

"The overrepresentation is real." They say this to sound honest, then spend six paragraphs reframing it away. The concession buys them credibility with lurkers. The reframe is where they do the actual work.

**How to survive it.** Quote the concession, ignore the reframe. "Even they admit the overrepresentation is real." Their honesty becomes your headline. They hate this, which is why some of the smarter ones have started qualifying their concessions more carefully. The more qualifications they add, the less powerful the concession sounds, which means they're already adapting.

### The Calm Frame

They never lose their temper. They write like they're mildly amused. When you escalate, they stay flat. The contrast makes you look unhinged and them look in control. This is deliberate.

**How to survive it.** Match it. The moment you use caps, exclamation marks, or personal insults, you've confirmed their frame. Stay dry. Stay factual. If you can't stay calm, don't reply. Come back an hour later.

### The Parallel Trap

"Men commit 75% of crime at 49% of the population. By your logic, we should deport men." They apply your reasoning to a group you belong to, then watch you struggle to explain why it's different.

**How to survive it.** The difference is policy-relevant, and you have to say it in ten words or fewer. "Men can't be deported. Visa policy is a choice. That's the difference." If it takes three paragraphs, you've already lost the lurker.

## Their Modes

### Bait Mode

Goal: provoke a long, emotional response that makes the progressive look obsessed. Post something short, provocative, and slightly wrong. Wait. When they write 500 words correcting you, you've won. The lurker sees someone who spent their afternoon arguing with a two-line comment.

**When to use it.** When you're outnumbered in a thread and can't win on volume. Their effort becomes your evidence that the topic is more controversial than it appears.

**Risk.** A skilled opponent will match your brevity. If they reply with three lines that make you look stupid, bait mode backfired. Only bait when you're confident they'll over-invest.

### Flood Mode

Goal: change the visible consensus. Five comments from five accounts saying variations of the same thing creates the impression that "everyone" agrees. Quality doesn't matter. Quantity creates atmosphere.

**Risk.** They've started documenting coordination patterns. Same talking points across communities within hours gets flagged. Coordinate the message, not the wording.

### Reasonable Mode

Goal: win the undecided reader by sounding moderate. "I'm not against immigration, I just think we need to look at the numbers honestly." This is the default mode. "I'm just concerned" is the safest possible positioning.

**Risk.** Good opponents will call this "concern trolling" and they'll be partially right. The defence is consistency. If your comment history is full of bait-mode posts and one reasonable-mode comment, you'll get called out.

### Victim Mode

Goal: reframe pushback as persecution. "So I can't even ask questions anymore?" "Everyone who disagrees with you is a Nazi, right?" "Free speech is dead." Transform the thread from "you said something wrong" to "they're trying to silence dissent."

**When to use it.** When you're losing on substance and need to change the frame. Works best when the opponent has been slightly aggressive. Any insult, any condescension gives you material.

**Risk.** Overuse makes you look fragile, not persecuted. And the best opponents won't give you the aggression you need. Victim mode requires their emotional escalation as fuel. If they stay calm and factual, you have nothing to react to.

## Where They're Actually Effective

This is the section the right-wing operative doesn't want to write. But losing threads you should win is worse than admitting you have gaps.

**They over-invest.** Sometimes that's a weakness. But the people who treat every thread like a battle are the ones who read the full study and know what "controlled for socioeconomic status" means. When they turn your source against you, you can't turn it back because you didn't read it that carefully. This is a capability gap, not a framing problem.

**They sound academic.** "The seven-year longitudinal study from the Kriminologisches Forschungsinstitut Niedersachsen demonstrates..." Nobody talks like this. Common sense beats expertise in the court of public opinion, not because it's right, but because it's accessible. But a growing number of them have learned to translate findings into plain language. When they do, they're harder to dismiss.

**They concede points.** And the concessions get quoted out of context. Every "the overrepresentation is real" becomes a headline. But the smarter ones have adapted. Their concessions now come with built-in context that's harder to strip away.

**They're predictable.** Same studies, same structural-causes argument, same "correlation isn't causation" rebuttal. Predictability means counters can be pre-loaded. But it also means reliability. Lurkers who see the same well-sourced rebuttal across three threads start to trust it.

**They care about being right.** Versus caring about being persuasive. Different games. But the ones who've learned to be concise about being right are genuinely dangerous. A correct three-line reply with one source beats a wrong ten-line reply every time.

**They won't say the uncomfortable thing.** Their reframing always leads back to "more integration funding, faster work permits, better housing." The lurker scared about crime in their neighbourhood doesn't want to hear about housing policy. But the best of them have found ways to acknowledge fear without validating the policy conclusions that follow.

**They're getting better faster than we are.** Frameworks, audits on their own numbers before posting, shared checklists. Volume and speed are advantages that erode the moment the other side starts coordinating too.

## What This Means for You

If you do counter-speech (or just argue with bad-faith accounts in comment sections), this document is a mirror. It shows you what the other side sees when they look at you. Some of it is flattering. Some of it should make you uncomfortable.

The operational takeaways are simple. Recognise the modes. Two-line provocation followed by silence is bait mode. Don't write 500 words in response. Five accounts echoing the same talking point is flood mode. Pick the strongest version and reply once. Sudden shift to "so I can't even ask questions?" is victim mode, and it only works if you gave them ammunition. Stay calm, stay factual, stay boring.

Fix the weaknesses they've identified. If you concede a point, build the context into the concession so it can't be stripped out. If you cite a source, read the whole thing. If you write long replies, ask whether the lurker (who won't read past paragraph two) gets the point from your first three sentences. Vary your approach.

Remember who the audience is. Not the person you're replying to. The hundred people reading silently. Everything you write is for them. The right-wing operative knows this. The question is whether you act on it as consistently as they do.
