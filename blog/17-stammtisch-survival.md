# Stammtisch Survival: How to Handle Political Arguments at Dinner

Online counter-speech and dinner table arguments are different sports. Online, you write for the silent audience and your opponent is a prop. At a family dinner, your opponent is your uncle, and you'll see him at Christmas. The stakes are personal, the format is verbal, and nobody is keeping score except your own nervous system.

Different rules apply. Here's what actually works when someone drops AfD talking points between the main course and dessert.

## The 3-Sentence Rule

At a Stammtisch (the German tradition of regulars meeting at a pub or kitchen table), attention spans are short and emotions run hot. If you can't make your point in three sentences, you've already lost the room.

The formula: one fact, one authority, one question.

"The AfD voted against the minimum wage increase in the Bundestag. That's not my opinion, that's the parliamentary record. What exactly are they doing for the working class?"

That's it. No six-point rebuttal. No source dump. One concrete fact the other person didn't know, one reference to an unchallengeable authority, one question that forces them to think. Then stop talking and let the silence do the work.

## Acknowledge First, Fact Second

The single biggest mistake in face-to-face political conversations is leading with "you're wrong." The research on persuasion (deep canvassing studies, exit counsellor interviews, street epistemology) all point the same direction: start by validating the emotion, then introduce the fact.

"I get the frustration. The government has made a mess of things, no argument there. But did you know the AfD voted against the Kindergeld increase? Against the Mietpreisbremse? In ten years, they haven't passed a single law."

The first sentence costs you nothing and buys you everything. The person across the table feels heard instead of attacked. Their defences drop for exactly long enough to let the fact land.

Skip this step and you get the Trotzreflex (defiance reflex). People don't process information they receive while feeling threatened.

## Questions Beat Statements

Street epistemology (a conversational technique developed for exactly these situations) has one core insight: asking "how do you know that?" is more powerful than saying "that's not true."

When someone says "the AfD is the only party that cares about normal people," don't counter with evidence. Ask: "What has changed for you since they entered parliament?" Then wait. Genuinely wait. Don't fill the silence.

Most people have never been asked this question. The honest answer is "nothing," and they know it. The question forces them to confront the gap between the promise and the delivery without you having to point it out. Self-generated doubt sticks. Externally imposed doubt bounces.

Good follow-up questions: "What would they need to do for you to reconsider?" or "If the AfD had a majority, what law would they pass first?" These aren't gotcha questions. They're invitations to think concretely, which is the one thing populist slogans can't survive.

## Deep Canvassing: The Ten-Minute Version

Deep canvassing is a research-backed technique originally developed for door-to-door conversations about contentious topics. A single ten-minute conversation can reduce prejudice for at least three months (Science, 2016). The key: share a personal story and ask for theirs.

"My colleague is Syrian. He's been here eight years, speaks fluent German, pays taxes, coaches his kid's football team. Sellner's 'remigration' plan calls people like him 'not assimilated.' That word has a specific meaning when you know the person it's aimed at. Do you know anyone it would affect?"

Abstractions are easy to dismiss. People you've met are not.

## What to Do With Whataboutism

"But the other parties are just as bad!" is the most common deflection at any German dinner table. Don't defend the other parties. That's the trap.

Instead: "You're right, the others have problems too. But the AfD had 152 seats for ten years and passed zero laws. Zero. How do they solve your problem?"

## When to Stop

Face-to-face conversations have a natural limit. You can feel it: the other person's jaw tightens, their voice gets louder, they start repeating the same point for the third time. These are signals that the rational conversation is over and the identity defence has kicked in.

At that point, pushing harder does damage. Not to your argument (that ship has sailed) but to the relationship. And the relationship is what keeps the door open for the next conversation, and the one after that.

Exit gracefully: "We're going in circles. I've said my piece, you've said yours. Let's get another beer."

The Nantes study on cult recovery found that 58% of people who left extremist groups said a conversation with a friend or family member was the trigger. Not the first conversation, and not the loudest one. You're planting a seed, not harvesting a crop.

## The Cheat Sheet

Keep these in your back pocket for any Stammtisch:

- AfD voted against minimum wage, Kindergeld, and Mietpreisbremse (source: Bundestag voting records)
- Ten years in parliament, zero laws passed (source: DGB analysis)
- Dexit would cost 690 billion euros and 2.5 million jobs (source: IW Koeln, the employers' think tank, not a left-wing outfit)
- Every moderate leader has been pushed out: Lucke, Petry, Meuthen (source: their own public statements)

Each one fits in a single sentence and comes from a source the other person can't dismiss as left-wing.
