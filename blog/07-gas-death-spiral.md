# The Gas Death Spiral

The CDU "abolished" Habeck's heating law. They kept the CO2 price. They kept the grid fees. They kept every cost driver that makes gas more expensive each year. The only thing they removed was the mechanism that would have protected renters from getting locked into a dying system.

This is not an energy policy story. It is a class story wearing an energy policy costume.

## What actually happened

In February 2026, the new CDU-SPD coalition scrapped the 65% renewable rule from Germany's Building Energy Act. Oil and gas boilers are unrestricted again. The advisory requirement for new heating installations is gone. Jens Spahn announced that "the boiler room is a private matter again."

What Spahn left out: the costs will not be private. They never were.

The CDU rebranded the law as the "Building Modernisation Act," kept the climate targets for the building sector, and kept the CO2 pricing mechanism that makes gas progressively more expensive. The physics did not change. The name of the law did.

## The spiral, explained

Germany operates roughly 500,000 kilometres of gas pipeline. That infrastructure has fixed costs regardless of how many customers use it. The DIW Berlin warned in 2024 that as customers leave the gas grid, network fees for the remaining users could rise by a factor of ten over twenty years.

The mechanism is straightforward. Homeowners who can do the maths are switching to heat pumps (the economics already favour it). Fewer gas customers means the same infrastructure costs spread across fewer accounts. Fees rise. More homeowners leave. Fees rise again.

This is a death spiral, and it has a specific victim: anyone who cannot choose their own heating system. Renters. The landlord picks the cheapest boiler to install (gas, a few thousand euros). The tenant pays the operating costs for fifteen to twenty years. Rising CO2 levies, rising grid fees, rising gas commodity prices. No exit.

The CO2 price alone tells the story. It sat at 25 euros per tonne in 2021. It hit 55 euros in 2025. From 2028, German gas falls under EU emissions trading, where projections run above 80 euros. ZEW estimates suggest gas households could face up to 1,000 euros in additional annual CO2 costs by 2030. Every year the price only goes one direction.

## The campaign that caused the damage

Heat pump sales in Germany crashed 46% in 2024. Not because the technology failed. Not because costs were prohibitive. Because the CDU ran one of the most effective disinformation campaigns in recent German politics.

"Heating hammer." "Forced heat pumps." "Totalitarian interference in your boiler room." BILD ran the framing. The CDU amplified it. Consumers froze. Stiebel Eltron, one of Germany's largest heat pump manufacturers, went on short-time work. The industry had already retooled for the transition. The CDU's campaign created an investment blockade that cost thousands of skilled trades jobs.

What Habeck actually proposed: a phased transition, technology-neutral (heat pumps, district heating, pellets, hybrids all qualified), with subsidies covering 50 to 70% of costs, transition periods extending into the 2040s, and exemptions for existing functional systems. The 65% rule only kicked in after a municipality completed its local heating plan.

None of that survived contact with a tabloid headline.

## The myths that keep people trapped

The most persistent claim: heat pumps only work in new builds with underfloor heating. Fraunhofer ISE tested 77 heat pumps in existing homes built between 1826 and 2001. Nearly 90% of the households heated without underfloor heating. CO2 emissions ran 64% below comparable gas boilers. Backup heating elements accounted for just 1.9% of total heating output.

The cost myth is equally stubborn. An air-source heat pump for a detached house runs around 30,000 euros before subsidies. After the 50 to 70% grant, that drops to 9,000 to 15,000 euros, roughly the same as a new gas boiler. An RWTH Aachen study with Octopus Energy found that the price gap between Germany and the UK is driven by Germany's 19% VAT on heat pumps (the UK charges 0%), percentage-based subsidies that inflate rather than compress prices, and over-regulated installation requirements. Japanese top-tier units cost under 5,000 euros at the manufacturer. Barely any German installer offers them. Market failure, not physics.

Annual running costs tell the rest. A gas-heated detached house using 20,000 kWh pays roughly 2,000 euros for gas plus 240 to 311 euros in CO2 levies, with grid fees climbing. A heat pump at the same output on a heat pump tariff costs around 1,140 euros. The gap exceeds 1,000 euros per year and widens every year the CO2 price rises.

The consumer advice centre (Verbraucherzentrale) already advises against installing new gas boilers.

## Who wins, who loses

Homeowners with capital and information are switching regardless of what the law says. They can read a spreadsheet. The CDU's "liberation" changes nothing for them.

Landlords win. They install the cheapest heating system available (gas), pass operating costs to tenants, and pocket the difference between a 5,000-euro gas boiler and a 15,000-euro heat pump investment they no longer need to make.

Renters lose. They inherit a gas connection they did not choose, in a grid that is haemorrhaging customers, under a CO2 price regime that only escalates. They cannot access the renovation subsidies (those are for property owners). They cannot switch their heating system. They pay the bills.

Habeck's 65% rule was never about punishing homeowners. It was about preventing landlords from externalising fossil fuel costs onto tenants who have no alternative. The CDU removed that protection and called it freedom.

## The quiet part

Germany's building sector is already missing its climate targets. The Federal Administrative Court ordered the government to strengthen its climate programme in January 2026. At least two independent legal opinions (including from environmental lawyer Roda Verheyen) conclude that rolling back the 65% rule violates the constitutional climate ruling of 2021, which established that the state must protect future generations' freedom by acting on emissions now.

The CDU kept the constitutional obligation. They just removed the tool that would have met it.

Gas customers are now locked into a shrinking network, facing rising fees, with no legal protection and no affordable exit. The CDU calls this "technology neutrality." The DIW calls it a death spiral. The people stuck inside it will call it something else entirely.
